# CTA Writing

## Purpose
Call-to-action buttons and links should minimize anxieties, avoid suggesting work, and match the message that brought readers to the content.

## Core Principle
Every piece of content should have one goal, reflected in one primary CTA. That CTA can be repeated, but it should always support the same goal.

## CTA Rules

1. Every piece of content should have one goal, reflected in the CTA
2. Maximum of one CTA per piece of content (can be repeated)
3. Both CTAs must support the same goal
4. Passive CTAs (social icons, navigation) are still CTAs—don't include unless they're the primary goal

## Technique 1: Minimize Anxieties

Think of your CTA as a closed door. Readers are on a page they know, and the CTA invites them to leave that page for a new, unknown space.

**What readers worry about:**
- What's really on the other side?
- Will they be surprised by what follows?
- Is the potential benefit worth the risk?

**How to minimize anxieties:**

**Bad:** "Get my quotes" (ambiguous—what do I have to do? Will I have to pay?)

**Good:** "Show me my quotes" (assures them quotes will be displayed right after clicking)

**Examples:**
- "Show me my quotes" vs "Get my quotes" (10.3% lift)
- "Try it free" vs "Add it to WordPress now" (15% lift)

## Technique 2: Don't Introduce Work

CTAs often lead to experiences that involve work. Don't remind readers of the work they'll have to do.

**Bad:** "Add It to WordPress Now" (suggests work is about to follow)

**Good:** "Try It Free Now" (focuses on the benefit, not the work)

**Rule:** People don't want to be reminded of the work that must be done, even if they'll still have to do it.

## Technique 3: Match Headline to CTA

Repeat the verb-phrase from your headline in your CTA for consistency.

**Example:**
- **Headline:** "Learn TTPA Compliance in 15 Minutes"
- **CTA:** "Learn TTPA Compliance Now"

**Result:** 24% increase in clicks when button copy matched headline copy

## When to Use

### Blog Posts
- Place CTAs after key sections, not just at the end
- Use anxiety-reducing language: "Download free," "No email required"
- Match CTA to the article's main promise

### Social Media Posts
- Use clear, action-oriented CTAs: "Read more," "Download now," "Learn how"
- Include anxiety reducers: "Free," "Quick tip," "2-minute read"
- Match CTA to the post's message

### Glossary Entries
- Use educational CTAs: "Learn more," "See examples," "Read the full guide"
- Avoid promotional language in glossary CTAs

## Pro Tip: Form Fields Are Part of Your CTA

If you have an opt-in form, it's part of your CTA. The fewer fields, the more sign-ups—but more fields mean more qualified leads.

**Rule:** Ask people to complete only as much work as is fair in exchange for your offering, and make completing the form highly desirable with a low-anxiety, low-effort button.

## Key Takeaway

Write CTAs that minimize anxieties, avoid suggesting work, and match the message that brought readers to your content. Focus on the benefit of clicking, not the work that follows. Make it easy and desirable to take action.
