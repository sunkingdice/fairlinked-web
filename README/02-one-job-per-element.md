# One Job Per Element

## Purpose
Each element of your content has one specific job. Don't make one element do multiple jobs—it will fail at all of them.

## Core Principle
Think of content elements as workers on an assembly line. Every worker has her own job. If one job isn't done well, it makes it impossible for the other workers down the line to do their jobs.

## Element Jobs

### Headline
**Job:** Keep arriving readers on the page (or keep them reading)

### Subhead
**Job:** Move readers to the body copy

### Body Copy
**Job:** Directly support the page goal

### Social Proof
**Job:** Turn naysayers into believers

### Call to Action (CTA)
**Job:** Get clicked/shared/acted upon

## When to Use

### Blog Posts
- **Headline:** Keep readers reading the article
- **Opening paragraph:** Move readers into the body
- **Body sections:** Support the article's goal (educate, persuade, inform)
- **CTA:** Get readers to take action (download, subscribe, share)

### Social Media Posts
- **Opening line:** Keep followers reading the post
- **Body:** Support the post's goal (inform, entertain, persuade)
- **CTA:** Get followers to click, share, or comment

### Glossary Entries
- **Term/Title:** Keep readers reading the definition
- **Definition:** Support the goal (educate, clarify)
- **Examples:** Turn skeptics into believers (show it works)

## The Open Loop Technique

If you're driving to content from another source (email, social post, ad), write that source in such a way that visitors are given the first half of an important or intriguing piece of information, with the promise of "closing the loop" in the main content.

**Example:**
- **Email subject:** "The one TTPA mistake that costs companies €50K..."
- **Blog headline:** "...and how to avoid it in 3 simple steps"

## Pro Tip: Match Headline to CTA

Try repeating the verb-phrase from your headline in your CTA. This creates consistency and can increase clicks.

**Example:**
- **Headline:** "Learn TTPA Compliance in 15 Minutes"
- **CTA:** "Learn TTPA Compliance Now"

## Key Takeaway

Can each element do more than the work they're responsible for? Sure. But if you make them responsible for jobs that should be done further down the line, you set them up for failure. Focus each element on its one job.
