# Emotions and Feelings in Copy

## Purpose
Tap into customer emotions to build familiarity and trust that gets people to buy. In the age of AI, humanity is the most important differentiator.

## Core Principle
People have feelings. Whether they're B2B or B2C, technical or non-technical, everyone has emotions that drive decisions. Nobody takes action when things are fine—pain is motivating.

## The 6 Core Feelings

### 1. Frustration / Pain / Fear / Anger

**Why to Use:**
- Nobody takes action when things are totally fine
- Pain is motivating
- Even "vitamin" products use pain to sell themselves
- You need to make people feel bad because nobody takes action if things are fine

**Where to Use:**
- Ads (to get attention)
- Social post hooks (people love to see problems they're having)
- At the tops of landing pages and emails (especially with colder audiences)
- Show them you understand their pain before offering solutions

**What It Looks Like:**
- "It sucks when..."
- "Tired of... / Sick of... / Done with..."
- "Don't you hate this?"
- "We've all been there..."
- "We know your pain"

**Example:**
- "We've all been there. Hit send too soon. Shared the wrong link. Notice a typo after you already went to the printer. We know your pain."

**Do:**
- Address pain honestly and specifically
- Make people feel bad about something they can solve with your product

**Don't:**
- Over-exaggerate the problem
- Make people feel bad about things they can't change (weight, income, self-esteem)

### 2. Skepticism

**Why to Use:**
- Because they're feeling it (especially technical audiences, developers)
- With hype comes skepticism
- Need to find ways to promote without promotion
- Meet your reader where they are

**Where to Use:**
- Anytime your reader could reasonably say "Sure, but..."
- Competitor comparison pages
- Sales/nurture emails
- Parenthetical asides (anywhere)

**What It Looks Like:**
- "We know, we know..."
- "You're probably thinking..."
- "Do [thing they want] without [thing they hate]"
- "You think [objection]. But actually..."
- "Even if..."
- Parenthetical asides: "(Looking at you, California)"

**Example:**
- "Send mandatory emails to everyone, even to contacts who are unsubscribed from optional content."
- "We're not saying we're the best. We're saying the next best thing."

**Pro Tip:**
Parenthetical asides cue the eyeball—they're like whispering into your reader's ear with additional information they might need.

### 3. Hope

**Why to Use:**
- To clue people in that things can be different (and probably better)
- As an on-ramp to excitement (don't go from zero to 100)
- Balance out pain—after hammering pain, show it can get better

**Where to Use:**
- Wherever you talk about all the awesome things they'll be able to do with your product
- Body copy, headlines, emails
- Definitely in nurture sequences if trying to get someone to upgrade

**What It Looks Like:**
- "Imagine if you could..."
- "You'll love..."
- "Wouldn't it be awesome to..."
- "Run tournaments that players will love"
- "A time tracking tool developers will love"

**Key Principle:**
Hope is before the amazing thing happens. Hope is before they sign up. Hope is before they have their activation aha moment.

### 4. Excitement

**Why to Use:**
- Get users amped up so they're ready to take action
- Reaffirm they've made the right choice after they've just taken an action
- If you're not excited, why would they be?

**Where to Use:**
- Painting a picture of their bright future (nurturing flows, tops and bottoms of landing pages)
- Celebrating onboarding milestones
- Emails from the founder
- CTAs (calls to action should be exciting)

**What It Looks Like:**
- "Finally—now you can..."
- "You're about to [outcome they want]"
- "Heck yes, give me [thing]"
- "Congrats on [awesome choice]"
- "Make [cool thing] happen now"
- "You did it!"
- Speed/immediacy: "Schedule events at the speed of thought"
- "Put me into this program right now"

**Don't Use:**
- "Request a demo" (adds friction)
- "Learn more" (nobody wants to learn more)
- Generic, boring CTAs

**Do Use Instead:**
- "Book a quick, friendly 15-minute demo"
- "Improve retention now" (call to value, not call to action)
- "Smash that button"

**Pro Tip:**
Speed is exciting. Immediacy is exciting. But you need context—why is speed good? What does it mean?

**Key Principle:**
You need hope as an on-ramp to excitement. Otherwise, it's like going from zero to 100, and people are like, "Whoa, tone it down."

### 5. Flattery

**Why to Use:**
- People like feeling smart, savvy, and on top of their shit
- If we can make them feel good, they will come back, stay, and won't get mad when we do something wrong
- Makes oops emails more forgivable

**Where to Use:**
- In cold emails (sparingly—avoid sycophantic)
- When writing copy directly to one ICP (defining them in the copy)
- When identifying the reader's role, position, or responsibilities

**What It Looks Like:**
- "You're not stupid, so you know this is the way to do things"
- "Not everyone cares (or even knows) the difference between X and Y, but you do"
- "As an experienced [ROLE], you know that..."
- "You do business in multiple currencies, so you get it"
- Self-deprecation + flattery combo (charm and disarm method)

**Example:**
- "Good morning, Lianna. This is very non-Swedish of me, reaching out like this to a complete rock star. Despite being a complete noob, I decided to take us live on Product Hunt today."

**Pro Tip:**
A little goes a long way, especially if you're not already doing this. Avoid being sycophantic in cold outreach.

### 6. Confidence

**Why to Use:**
- To inspire confidence in them (same deal as excitement—it's contagious)
- Show that we believe in what we're doing
- Make them believe the tool won't disappear tomorrow

**Where to Use:**
- Headlines (hero headline on homepage or landing page)
- Pricing page
- Onboarding emails (you're so sure you can help them get the result)
- Any promises or claims
- Free trial offers
- FAQs (showing you anticipate their questions)

**What It Looks Like:**
- Clear statements (often short sentences)
- FAQs (because you anticipate their questions)
- "Did we miss something? Reach out. Tell us we're wrong."
- Unique, bold, or specific claims
- Social proof and trust signals

**Example:**
- "Save four hours per person every single week" (the "every single week" is the confidence)
- "See results from your first ad" (confident claim you better be able to back up)
- "Trusted by 25,000+ American small business owners"

**Types of Social Proof/Trust Signals:**
- Reviews on G2, Capterra, TrustPilot
- Testimonials
- Case studies
- Certifications
- Client/customer logos
- Press coverage
- Guarantees
- Numbers: customers served, years in business, revenue generated

**Key Principle:**
Confidence is a feeling we feel and pass on to the reader. If you're not confident, they won't be either.

## Key Principles

### 1. Pain is Motivating
Nobody takes action when things are totally fine. You need to make people feel bad because nobody takes action if things are fine. But you don't have to overplay it.

### 2. Hope as On-Ramp to Excitement
Don't go from zero to 100. Use hope to transition from pain to excitement. Otherwise, it looks weird and people will say, "Whoa, tone it down."

### 3. Balance Pain with Positive Emotions
After hammering pain, it's important to balance out with some hope because we don't want to just make people feel so bad that they don't do anything. Show them it can get better.

### 4. A Little Goes a Long Way
If you're scared to use emotion in your copy (especially if you have stakeholders who want you to sound big and fancy and boring), just a little bit goes a long way.

### 5. If You Want Them Excited, You Gotta Be Excited
If you're not excited, why would they be? It's not fun to buy from a tool that's like, "I don't know, I guess you could use it. Sure, fine."

## When to Use

### Blog Posts
- **Frustration:** Opening paragraphs, especially for cold audiences
- **Skepticism:** Address objections throughout, use parenthetical asides
- **Hope:** When describing benefits and outcomes
- **Excitement:** Near CTAs, in closing paragraphs
- **Flattery:** When defining your reader's role or expertise
- **Confidence:** In headlines, claims, and social proof

### Social Media Posts
- **Frustration:** In hooks (people love to see problems they're having)
- **Skepticism:** Address common objections in the post
- **Hope:** Paint picture of better future
- **Excitement:** In CTAs, celebrating milestones
- **Flattery:** When speaking directly to your ICP
- **Confidence:** In bold claims and social proof

### Emails
- **Frustration:** Top of emails, especially with colder audiences
- **Skepticism:** In nurture sequences, sales emails
- **Hope:** In nurture sequences when trying to get upgrades
- **Excitement:** Emails from founder, onboarding emails, milestone celebrations
- **Flattery:** In cold emails (sparingly), when identifying reader
- **Confidence:** In promises, claims, social proof

### Landing Pages
- **Frustration:** At the top, especially for cold audiences
- **Skepticism:** Throughout, in competitor comparisons, parenthetical asides
- **Hope:** In body copy describing benefits
- **Excitement:** At tops and bottoms, in CTAs
- **Flattery:** When defining ICP
- **Confidence:** In headlines, pricing page, social proof

## Common Mistakes

### 1. Over-Exaggerating Pain
**Don't:**
- Make the problem seem worse than it is

**Do:**
- Address pain honestly and specifically

### 2. Making People Feel Bad About Things They Can't Change
**Don't:**
- Focus on weight, income, self-esteem issues

**Do:**
- Focus on problems they can solve with your product

### 3. Going Zero to 100 (No Hope Before Excitement)
**Don't:**
- Jump straight from pain to excitement

**Do:**
- Use hope as an on-ramp to excitement

### 4. Generic, Boring CTAs
**Don't:**
- "Request a demo" (adds friction)
- "Learn more" (nobody wants to learn more)

**Do:**
- "Book a quick, friendly 15-minute demo"
- "Improve retention now" (call to value)

### 5. Not Being Excited Yourself
**Don't:**
- "I guess you could use it. Sure, fine."

**Do:**
- Show genuine excitement about your product

## Pro Tips

### Parenthetical Asides
Parenthetical asides cue the eyeball—they're like whispering into your reader's ear with additional information. Use them to address skepticism naturally.

### Call to Value vs Call to Action
- **Call to Action:** "Sign up now" (what they're going to do)
- **Call to Value:** "Improve retention now" (what they're going to get)

### Speed Needs Context
Speed is exciting, but you need context. "Engaged in under three minutes" is meaningless without context. Why is three minutes good? What are agents?

### Social Proof Placement
Place social proof strategically:
- Near objections (to counter skepticism)
- Near CTAs (to provide final push)
- Throughout content (to build credibility continuously)

## Key Takeaway

You have to use pain and anxiety—it's necessary. But you don't have to overplay it. Lead into excitement with hope. If you want them to be excited, you have to be excited. Flatter their asses (make them feel special). And honest confidence is key. In the age of AI, humanity is the most important differentiator.
