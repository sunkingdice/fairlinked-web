# Benefits vs Features

## Purpose
Body copy should focus on the most powerful benefits, results, and outcomes—not every possible benefit or a laundry list of features.

## Core Principle
You only need as many benefits as are necessary to move the reader—in their current state of awareness—toward the call to action.

## Benefits vs Features

### Features
What your product or service does or has.

**Examples:**
- "Automated compliance tracking"
- "24/7 support"
- "Cloud-based platform"

### Benefits
What the reader gets or achieves from those features.

**Examples:**
- "Save 10 hours per week on compliance tracking"
- "Get help whenever you need it, day or night"
- "Access your compliance data from anywhere"

## How to Select the Most Powerful Benefits

### 1. Work Backward from Your Goal
Start with your page goal, identify objections, then determine which benefits address those objections.

### 2. Consider Awareness Level
- **Pain Aware:** Focus on benefits that solve their pain
- **Solution Aware:** Focus on benefits that differentiate you
- **Product Aware:** Focus on benefits that prove you're the best choice
- **Most Aware:** Focus on benefits related to the specific offer

### 3. Use Customer Language
The benefits customers mention most frequently are likely your strongest selling points.

## How Many Benefits to Include

**Rule of thumb:** Include only the benefits necessary to move the reader toward your goal.

**Too many benefits:** Creates TMI (Too Much Information) and dilutes your message
**Too few benefits:** May not address enough objections or anxieties

**Example of too many:**
- Benefit 1: Save time
- Benefit 2: Reduce costs
- Benefit 3: Improve accuracy
- Benefit 4: Increase efficiency
- Benefit 5: Enhance security
- Benefit 6: Simplify processes
- Benefit 7: Streamline workflows
- Benefit 8: Boost productivity

**Better approach:** Focus on the 2-3 most powerful benefits that directly support your goal.

## When to Use

### Blog Posts
- Focus body copy on benefits that support the article's goal
- Use features to support benefits, not replace them
- Structure: Benefit → How (feature) → Result

**Example:**
- "Save 10 hours per week on compliance tracking (benefit) with our automated system (feature) so you can focus on growing your business (result)"

### Social Media Posts
- Lead with the most powerful benefit
- Use features as supporting details if needed
- Keep it concise—one main benefit per post

### Glossary Entries
- Focus on benefits of understanding the term
- Use features only if they help explain the concept
- Keep it educational, not promotional

## Pro Tip: The Power of Specificity

Specific benefits outperform vague ones.

**Vague:** "Save time on compliance"
**Specific:** "Save 10 hours per week on compliance tracking"

**Vague:** "Reduce costs"
**Specific:** "Reduce compliance costs by up to 40%"

## Key Takeaway

Body copy should be built on the most powerful benefits, results, and outcomes—not every possible benefit or a checklist of features. Include only as many benefits as necessary to move the reader toward your goal.
