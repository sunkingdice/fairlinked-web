# Storytelling Frameworks

## Purpose
Select the right storytelling framework based on your reader's awareness level, content length, and emotional tone. Combine frameworks strategically for complex content.

## Core Principle
Different frameworks work for different situations. Match the framework to your reader's state of mind and your content goals. Most strong content uses multiple frameworks for different sections.

## Quick Decision Guide

### By Reader Awareness
- **Unaware of problem** → Use AIDA or BAB
- **Aware of problem, seeking solution** → Use PAS or PASTOR
- **Comparing solutions** → Use FAB or 4 Ps

### By Content Length
- **Short** (social, ads, headlines) → Use 4 Us, PAS, or BAB
- **Medium** (blog posts, emails) → Use AIDA, QUEST, or FAB
- **Long** (sales pages, guides) → Use PASTOR, StoryBrand, or Hero's Journey

### By Emotional Tone
- **Urgency/fear-based** → Use PAS
- **Aspirational/transformational** → Use BAB or Hero's Journey
- **Educational/trust-building** → Use QUEST or 4 Ps
- **Brand storytelling** → Use StoryBrand

## The 10 Frameworks

### 1. AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action)

**What It Is:**
The oldest and most universal copywriting framework. Guides readers through a logical progression from noticing your message to taking action.

**Structure:**
- **Attention**: Grab the reader with a headline, bold claim, or surprising fact
- **Interest**: Provide relevant information that keeps them reading
- **Desire**: Show benefits and outcomes that make them want what you offer
- **Action**: Tell them exactly what to do next

**Example:**
- **Attention**: "Your political ads might be illegal starting October 2025."
- **Interest**: "The EU's new Transparency and Targeting of Political Advertising regulation requires disclosure labels, audience data reporting, and a complete audit trail for every political message you publish."
- **Desire**: "Organizations using AdFairly's compliance platform reduced their TTPA preparation time by 80% and achieved full compliance within two weeks."
- **Action**: "Run a free compliance check now to see where you stand."

**When to Use:**
- Landing pages and homepages
- Email marketing sequences
- Product announcements
- General awareness campaigns
- When readers may not know they have a problem

**When NOT to Use:**
- When reader is already problem-aware and comparing solutions
- For technical documentation or how-to guides
- When urgency is critical (use PAS instead)
- For case studies or success stories (use Hero's Journey instead)

**Strengths:**
- Works for almost any content type
- Easy to learn and apply
- Logical flow that matches how people make decisions
- Flexible length (works for tweets and sales pages)

**Weaknesses:**
- Can feel formulaic if overused
- Does not emphasize pain points strongly
- May not create enough urgency
- Less effective when reader already knows they need a solution

### 2. PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)

**What It Is:**
A three-step framework that identifies a problem, intensifies the emotional impact of that problem, then presents your solution as relief. Highly effective for pain-point marketing.

**Structure:**
- **Problem**: Identify the specific pain or frustration your reader faces
- **Agitate**: Make the problem feel more urgent, costly, or painful
- **Solution**: Present your offering as the clear path to relief

**Example:**
- **Problem**: "You have no idea if your political communications comply with TTPA."
- **Agitate**: "And you will not find out until an enforcement authority contacts you. By then, you are facing public disclosure of the violation, potential fines, and reputation damage during your most critical campaign period. Your legal team says you are probably fine. But 'probably' is not a compliance strategy."
- **Solution**: "AdFairly scans your political content against all 47 TTPA requirements and tells you exactly what needs fixing. Get your compliance report in under 5 minutes."

**When to Use:**
- When readers already feel pain but have not acted
- Time-sensitive or urgent situations
- Competitive markets where differentiation is hard
- Email subject lines and opening paragraphs
- Social media ads

**When NOT to Use:**
- Brand-building or awareness content
- When your audience is not experiencing active pain
- Educational or informational content
- When you want to inspire rather than alarm
- For products/services focused on growth rather than fixing problems

**Strengths:**
- Creates strong emotional response
- Works quickly (three steps)
- Highly effective for conversion
- Makes the cost of inaction clear

**Weaknesses:**
- Can feel manipulative if overdone
- Not suitable for positive/aspirational messaging
- May alienate readers who do not feel the pain
- Does not work well for unaware audiences

### 3. BAB (Before-After-Bridge)

**What It Is:**
A transformation-focused framework that shows readers their current state, paints a picture of their desired future state, and positions your product as the bridge between the two.

**Structure:**
- **Before**: Describe the current situation with its frustrations and limitations
- **After**: Paint a vivid picture of life after the problem is solved
- **Bridge**: Present your product/service as the path from Before to After

**Example:**
- **Before**: "Right now, your team spends hours manually checking each piece of political content. You are not sure if your disclosures are correct. You worry about what you might be missing. Every campaign launch comes with compliance anxiety."
- **After**: "Imagine publishing political content with complete confidence. Every disclosure automatically generated. Every requirement checked. A complete audit trail ready if anyone asks. Your team focused on the message, not the paperwork."
- **Bridge**: "AdFairly connects to your publishing workflow and handles TTPA compliance automatically. From manual checking to automated confidence in one integration."

**When to Use:**
- SaaS and software products
- Transformation-focused services (coaching, consulting)
- When showing contrast between old way and new way
- Aspirational messaging
- Case studies and testimonials

**When NOT to Use:**
- When immediate action is critical
- For audiences in crisis mode (they need solutions now, not visions)
- Technical or specification-focused content
- When the transformation is not dramatic or clear

**Strengths:**
- Focuses on positive outcomes rather than pain
- Creates clear mental picture of success
- Works well for products that enable transformation
- Less aggressive than PAS

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires reader to imagine a future state
- Less urgent than problem-focused frameworks
- May not work if the "before" is not painful enough
- Can feel generic without specific details

### 4. FAB (Features-Advantages-Benefits)

**What It Is:**
A product-focused framework that translates technical features into customer value. Moves from what something is, to why that matters, to what it does for the customer.

**Structure:**
- **Features**: What the product/service has or does (specifications)
- **Advantages**: Why those features are better than alternatives
- **Benefits**: What the customer gains (outcomes, feelings, results)

**Example:**
- **Feature**: "AdFairly includes automated hreflang tag generation for all 24 EU languages."
- **Advantage**: "Unlike manual translation workflows, you do not need to manage separate disclosure templates for each market."
- **Benefit**: "Launch pan-European political campaigns with compliant disclosures in every language, without additional translation costs or delays."

**When to Use:**
- Product pages and feature lists
- Technical audiences who want specifications
- Comparison content (us vs. competitors)
- Sales enablement materials
- When features are genuinely differentiating

**When NOT to Use:**
- Top-of-funnel awareness content
- When features are similar to competitors
- Emotional or brand storytelling
- When the audience does not understand technical terms

**Strengths:**
- Connects technical details to customer value
- Works well for complex products
- Provides concrete proof points
- Helps technical writers think about customer impact

**Weaknesses:**
- Can become feature-dumping if not careful
- Assumes reader cares about features
- Less emotional than other frameworks
- May not work for services without clear features

### 5. 4 Ps (Promise-Picture-Proof-Push)

**What It Is:**
A credibility-focused framework that makes a bold claim, visualizes the outcome, proves it works, then asks for action. Strong emphasis on evidence.

**Structure:**
- **Promise**: Make a clear, specific claim about what you deliver
- **Picture**: Help the reader visualize achieving that outcome
- **Proof**: Provide evidence (testimonials, data, case studies)
- **Push**: Call to action with urgency

**Example:**
- **Promise**: "Achieve full TTPA compliance in two weeks or less."
- **Picture**: "Picture your next political campaign launching without a single compliance question. Your legal team confident. Your communications team focused on messaging. Your audit trail complete before anyone asks for it."
- **Proof**: "The German Green Party reduced their compliance preparation from 6 weeks to 9 days. The Belgian Workers' Association found and fixed 23 disclosure gaps before enforcement began. Over 200 organizations across the EU trust AdFairly for TTPA compliance."
- **Push**: "Start your free compliance assessment today. See exactly where you stand in under 5 minutes."

**When to Use:**
- When you have strong proof points (data, testimonials)
- Sales pages and landing pages
- When making bold claims that need backing
- B2B content where credibility matters
- Competitive markets where trust is a differentiator

**When NOT to Use:**
- When you lack testimonials or case studies
- Early-stage products without track record
- Educational content
- When your claims cannot be proven

**Strengths:**
- Builds credibility through evidence
- Bold promises capture attention
- Visualization creates desire
- Clear call to action

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires actual proof (does not work without it)
- Can feel salesy if the push is too aggressive
- Needs strong testimonials or data
- Promise must be believable

### 6. PASTOR (Problem-Amplify-Solution-Transformation-Offer-Response)

**What It Is:**
An extended framework that expands PAS with transformation story, clear offer structure, and explicit response request. Designed for long-form sales copy.

**Structure:**
- **Problem**: Identify the problem your audience faces
- **Amplify**: Expand on the consequences of not solving it
- **Solution**: Present your solution and how it works
- **Transformation**: Show the before/after change with stories or testimonials
- **Offer**: Explain exactly what they get (deliverables, bonuses, guarantee)
- **Response**: Tell them how to buy/sign up with clear next steps

**Example:**
- **Problem**: "TTPA enforcement begins in October 2025, and most organizations have no systematic compliance process."
- **Amplify**: "Without proper compliance, you face public disclosure of violations in national ad repositories, potential fines, and the reputational damage of being listed as non-compliant during an election period. Your competitors who prepare now will have a significant advantage."
- **Solution**: "AdFairly provides end-to-end TTPA compliance automation. Our platform scans your content, generates required disclosures, and maintains the audit trail the regulation demands."
- **Transformation**: "The Danish Social Democrats went from zero TTPA readiness to full compliance in 11 days. 'We thought this would take months,' said their communications director. 'AdFairly made it straightforward.'"
- **Offer**: "AdFairly Professional includes unlimited content scans, automatic disclosure generation, audit trail storage, and dedicated support. EUR 299/month with a 30-day free trial."
- **Response**: "Start your free trial now. No credit card required. See your compliance status in under 5 minutes."

**When to Use:**
- Long-form sales pages
- Webinar scripts and presentations
- Email sequences (one element per email)
- High-ticket offers that need full explanation
- When you have transformation stories to share

**When NOT to Use:**
- Short-form content (social, ads, emails)
- Low-ticket or simple offers
- When you lack transformation stories
- Informational or educational content

**Strengths:**
- Complete framework covers all persuasion elements
- Transformation creates emotional connection
- Clear offer reduces confusion
- Works for complex or expensive products

**Weaknesses:**
- Too long for short content
- Requires strong transformation stories
- Can feel overwhelming if not well-paced
- Needs all six elements to work well

### 7. 4 Us (Urgent-Unique-Useful-Ultra-specific)

**What It Is:**
A headline and hook framework that ensures your opening captures attention by hitting four criteria. Primarily used for headlines, subject lines, and opening sentences.

**Structure:**
- **Urgent**: Creates time pressure or immediate relevance
- **Unique**: Offers something readers cannot get elsewhere
- **Useful**: Promises clear, practical value
- **Ultra-specific**: Uses concrete numbers, details, or specifics

**Example:**
- **Basic headline**: "TTPA Compliance Guide"
- **4 Us headline**: "The 9 TTPA Mistakes That Will Get You Fined (And How to Fix Them Before October)"
  - Urgent: "Before October"
  - Unique: "9 mistakes" (specific list not found elsewhere)
  - Useful: "How to fix them"
  - Ultra-specific: "9" and "October"

**When to Use:**
- Headlines and titles
- Email subject lines
- Social media hooks
- Ad copy openings
- Blog post titles

**When NOT to Use:**
- Body copy (use other frameworks)
- When subtlety is needed
- Academic or formal content
- When urgency would feel manipulative

**Strengths:**
- Quick checklist for improving any headline
- Dramatically increases click-through rates
- Easy to apply and test
- Works across all content types

**Weaknesses:**
- Only covers the hook, not full content
- Can create clickbait if overdone
- Not all four Us always possible
- Requires balance to avoid hype

### 8. QUEST (Qualify-Understand-Educate-Stimulate-Transition)

**What It Is:**
A content marketing framework that filters for the right audience, builds empathy, provides value, creates desire, and moves to action. Works well for educational and trust-building content.

**Structure:**
- **Qualify**: Filter for your target audience (who this is for)
- **Understand**: Show you understand their situation and challenges
- **Educate**: Provide valuable information that helps them
- **Stimulate**: Create desire for the solution or next step
- **Transition**: Move them to the action you want

**Example:**
- **Qualify**: "If your organization runs political advertising in the EU, or if you communicate about elections, referendums, or legislative issues, this guide is for you."
- **Understand**: "You are facing a regulation that is complex, new, and still being interpreted. You are not sure what counts as political advertising. You do not know if your current processes are sufficient. And October 2025 is approaching fast."
- **Educate**: "TTPA requires three core elements: transparency labels on all political ads, audience targeting disclosures, and a complete audit trail. Here is exactly how each requirement works and what it means for your organization..."
- **Stimulate**: "Organizations that prepare now will have a significant advantage. They will build compliance into their workflows rather than scrambling to retrofit it later."
- **Transition**: "Download our complete TTPA compliance checklist to see exactly where your organization stands."

**When to Use:**
- Blog posts and articles
- Educational content and guides
- Newsletter content
- Webinars and presentations
- Content for audiences new to a topic

**When NOT to Use:**
- Direct response advertising
- When readers already know the topic well
- Short-form content
- Urgency-driven campaigns

**Strengths:**
- Builds trust through education
- Filters for qualified readers
- Provides genuine value
- Natural flow from learning to action

**Weaknesses:**
- Slower path to conversion
- Requires actual educational content
- May lose readers who want quick answers
- Less direct than sales-focused frameworks

### 9. StoryBrand (Donald Miller)

**What It Is:**
A brand messaging framework based on the Hero's Journey. Positions the customer as the hero and your brand as the guide who helps them succeed. Creates clear, customer-focused messaging.

**Structure:**
1. **Character**: Your customer is the hero with a goal
2. **Problem**: They face an obstacle (external, internal, philosophical)
3. **Guide**: Your brand appears as a trusted advisor
4. **Plan**: You give them a clear path forward
5. **Action**: You call them to take a step
6. **Failure**: You show what happens if they do not act
7. **Success**: You show the positive outcome when they do

**Example:**
- **Character**: "You are a communications professional responsible for political messaging."
- **Problem**: "A new EU regulation threatens your ability to run campaigns effectively. Externally, you face complex requirements. Internally, you feel uncertain. Philosophically, you believe compliance should not require a legal team for every post."
- **Guide**: "AdFairly has helped over 200 organizations navigate TTPA. We understand the regulation because we helped shape the guidance around it."
- **Plan**: "Three steps: (1) Run our free compliance scan, (2) Get a prioritized fix list, (3) Implement with our automated tools."
- **Action**: "Start your free compliance scan now."
- **Failure**: "Without proper compliance, you risk public violation disclosures, fines, and campaign disruption."
- **Success**: "With AdFairly, you publish with confidence, knowing every requirement is handled."

**When to Use:**
- Website messaging and homepage
- Brand positioning and taglines
- Sales presentations
- Marketing strategy development
- When clarifying confused messaging

**When NOT to Use:**
- Single pieces of content (too broad)
- Technical documentation
- When you need specific copy, not strategy
- Short-form content

**Strengths:**
- Customer-centered (not brand-centered)
- Creates clear, simple messaging
- Works across all marketing materials
- Memorable story structure

**Weaknesses:**
- Framework, not a content template
- Requires understanding your customer deeply
- Can feel formulaic if applied too rigidly
- Takes time to develop properly

### 10. Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell)

**What It Is:**
The universal narrative structure identified by Joseph Campbell. Used in films, novels, and brand storytelling. Shows a character's transformation through challenge.

**Structure (Simplified for Marketing):**
1. **Ordinary World**: The hero's normal situation before the adventure
2. **Call to Adventure**: A problem or opportunity appears
3. **Refusal**: Initial hesitation or obstacles
4. **Meeting the Mentor**: A guide appears with wisdom
5. **Crossing the Threshold**: The hero commits to the journey
6. **Tests and Allies**: Challenges and helpers along the way
7. **The Ordeal**: The biggest challenge
8. **The Reward**: Success and transformation
9. **The Return**: The hero returns changed, with something to share

**Example (Case Study Structure):**
- **Ordinary World**: "The Belgian Workers' Association had run political campaigns for decades using the same processes."
- **Call to Adventure**: "Then TTPA was announced. Their existing workflow had no compliance checks, no disclosure system, no audit trail."
- **Refusal**: "Initially, they hoped the regulation would not apply to advocacy organizations. When they realized it did, they considered hiring a compliance consultant for six months."
- **Meeting the Mentor**: "Their communications director discovered AdFairly at a Brussels policy conference."
- **Crossing the Threshold**: "They decided to try the platform for their next campaign."
- **Tests**: "They discovered 23 disclosure gaps in their existing materials. Each one needed fixing before launch."
- **The Ordeal**: "With two weeks until campaign launch, they had to retrofit compliance into everything."
- **The Reward**: "They launched on time, fully compliant, with a complete audit trail."
- **The Return**: "Now they advise other advocacy organizations on TTPA preparation. 'We were terrified,' said the director. 'Now we have a system we trust.'"

**When to Use:**
- Case studies and customer stories
- Brand films and video content
- Founder stories and about pages
- Long-form content and ebooks
- Keynote presentations

**When NOT to Use:**
- Short-form content
- Technical or specification content
- When you lack real customer stories
- Direct response advertising

**Strengths:**
- Universal resonance (we all know this structure)
- Creates emotional connection
- Shows transformation powerfully
- Memorable and shareable

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires a real story with real transformation
- Too long for short content
- Can feel contrived if forced
- Needs strong narrative skills

## Combining Frameworks

For complex content, combine frameworks strategically:

### Blog Post Combination
- **4 Us** for the headline
- **QUEST** for the overall structure
- **PAS** or **BAB** for individual sections
- **AIDA** for the conclusion/CTA

### Sales Page Combination
- **4 Us** for the headline
- **PAS** for the opening
- **FAB** for feature sections
- **Hero's Journey** for case studies
- **4 Ps** for the close

### Email Sequence Combination
- Email 1: **BAB** (show transformation)
- Email 2: **PAS** (agitate the problem)
- Email 3: **FAB** (explain features)
- Email 4: **4 Ps** (proof and push)

### Website Combination
- **StoryBrand** for overall messaging architecture
- **AIDA** for homepage flow
- **FAB** for product pages
- **Hero's Journey** for case studies

## Quick Reference: One-Line Summaries

- **AIDA**: Guide them from attention to action
- **PAS**: Find the pain, twist it, solve it
- **BAB**: Show the transformation your product enables
- **FAB**: Connect features to customer benefits
- **4 Ps**: Promise, visualize, prove, ask
- **PASTOR**: Full sales argument with transformation story
- **4 Us**: Make headlines urgent, unique, useful, specific
- **QUEST**: Qualify, understand, educate, excite, convert
- **StoryBrand**: Make customer the hero, brand the guide
- **Hero's Journey**: Tell transformation stories that resonate

## Decision Checklist

When selecting a framework, ask:

1. **What is the reader's awareness level?**
   - Unaware → AIDA or BAB
   - Problem-aware → PAS or PASTOR
   - Comparing solutions → FAB or 4 Ps

2. **What is the content length?**
   - Short → 4 Us, PAS, or BAB
   - Medium → AIDA, QUEST, or FAB
   - Long → PASTOR, StoryBrand, or Hero's Journey

3. **What is the emotional tone?**
   - Urgency/fear → PAS
   - Aspirational → BAB or Hero's Journey
   - Educational → QUEST or 4 Ps
   - Brand storytelling → StoryBrand

4. **Do you have the required elements?**
   - 4 Ps needs proof
   - Hero's Journey needs a real story
   - FAB needs actual features
   - PASTOR needs transformation stories

5. **Should you combine frameworks?**
   - Most strong content uses multiple frameworks
   - Use different frameworks for different sections
   - Match framework to section purpose

## Key Takeaway

Frameworks are starting points, not rigid formulas. Match the framework to your reader's state of mind and your content goals. Most effective content combines multiple frameworks strategically. Avoid being formulaic—adapt frameworks to your specific content and audience.
